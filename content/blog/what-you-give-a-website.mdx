Open any website and, before you click a single thing, you've already handed it a pile of information about yourself. <Mark variant="highlight">You didn't agree to it. You didn't even notice.</Mark> It just happened, quietly, in the handshake.

I think about this a lot, because I care about building software that is honest with the people using it. So I want to show you the bill you already paid.

## You already paid, you just didn't see it

A page load is not a one-way street. To send you this page, your browser has to introduce itself, and that introduction says more than you'd guess.

<Callout type="note">
Just by arriving, a typical site can see your **IP address** (which maps to a rough city and your internet provider), your **device and operating system**, your **exact screen size**, your **timezone and languages**, your **CPU cores** and rough **memory**, your **connection type**, and often your **GPU**. Strung together, that combination is frequently unique enough to recognize you again without a single cookie.
</Callout>

None of that required a login. None of it asked. It's the cost of the handshake.

## Why most sites want it

For a lot of the web, you are not the customer, you are the inventory. That data feeds analytics, ad targeting, and <Mark variant="code">fingerprinting</Mark>, the practice of stitching those little signals into an ID that follows you around even when you clear cookies. It's not always sinister, but it's almost always invisible, and invisible is the part I have a problem with.

## What I chose for this site

So here's what this site does: <Mark variant="bold">no analytics, no tracking or advertising cookies, no accounts, no profiles.</Mark> A few preferences (the theme, the panel sizes, whether sound is on) are saved in your browser's local storage and never leave your device. There's nothing here trying to follow you home.

## The part I can't pretend away

But I'm not going to claim the handshake doesn't happen. Two ordinary things still see you. My host, Vercel, records your IP and basic request data in its logs, like every web server does. And the little location map on the home page loads its image from a maps provider, which means your IP gets sent to them to fetch that tile.

<Callout type="warning">
That's the honest catch: even a site built specifically to not track you still hands your IP to a third party just to draw a tiny map. Privacy on the web isn't a switch you flip. It's a pile of small defaults, and most of them aren't set in your favor.
</Callout>

## So I built you a mirror

That's why this site shows you your own footprint when you arrive, instead of quietly pocketing it. <Mark variant="highlight">Same data the whole web collects, except you actually get to see it.</Mark> Consent is just the difference between being shown the bill and being charged without one.

That's the whole idea behind how I try to build things: do the useful thing, in the open, and don't take what you didn't ask for.
